Wanderbench

An open spatial-navigation benchmark and RL training environment for vision-language models — 6,324 real-world Street View tasks across 1,122 US cities, with a single dense reward measuring how much of the start-to-goal distance the agent closed.

About the benchmark

The project

Modern vision-language models are remarkably good at describing physical scenes. Show them a static photo and ask "where am I?" and they'll often nail the country, city, sometimes the block. But the moment they have to act in physical space — "your goal is three blocks west, get there" — performance collapses. Spatial reasoning under sequential decision-making is the hole.

Wanderbench is a benchmark designed to surface that gap. Each task drops the agent into a real Mapillary panorama somewhere in the United States, hands it a goal latitude/longitude, and gives it the same primitives a human player would have in Street View: click on a road point to walk there, drag the pano to look around, scroll to zoom, open a map to orient, submit a guess when you've arrived.

The corpus is 6,324 tasks across 1,122 US cities, stratified across easy / medium / hard difficulty bands. The reward is dense and continuous — how much of the start-to-goal great-circle distance the agent closed by their final position — making the env equally usable for benchmarking (small reproducible subset) and for RL training (full corpus).

The environment

An agent sees a 1024 × 768 viewport rendered live from a Mapillary spherical panorama. A compass dial pins the camera's heading. A HUD line reports the current pano id, distance to goal, and last action. Pressing m swaps to an interactive OpenStreetMap view with start & goal pins, the explorable bbox, and the agent's current location.

The action space is six primitives, intentionally human-shaped:

tooleffect
move_cursorMove the on-screen cursor by a (direction, distance) vector.
mouse_down / mouse_upClick (no cursor movement between press & release) or drag.
open_map / close_mapToggle between street view and overhead map.
scroll_wheelZoom (FOV in pano; tile zoom in map).
submit_guessDeclare arrival. Ends the episode and scores final position vs goal.

Click semantics are deliberately faithful to Street View: a click on the street ahead teleports to that road point; a click on a building or the sky is a no-op. This forces the model to look at the image rather than blindly emit coordinates.

Reward design

A single terminal reward in [0, 1]:

pp = clip(1 − final_haversine_m / initial_haversine_m, 0, 1)

The denominator is the start-to-goal great-circle distance. The numerator is the great-circle distance from the agent's final pano coordinate to the goal coordinate. At the goal it's 1.0; halfway it's 0.5; stuck at start or past the start it's 0.0 (clipped). The walkability constraint is enforced by the simulator's step() at action time, not by the scorer — so the agent can't reach the goal by going through buildings, but the scorer doesn't care which road they ended on.

The first iteration of the reward mixed three terms — discrete reached_goal, continuous haversine progress, step-count efficiency. The second iteration switched to Dijkstra walking-distance through the world graph, on the intuition that "wrong side of the freeway" should hurt the score. After the first 60-model-task sweep that gave rise to three documented "scoring cliffs" (agents submitting within 20 m of the goal but scoring 0 because the OSM extract had their road graph-disconnected from the goal road), I reverted to haversine. The argument for haversine is empirical now: it matches what the per-turn HUD shows the agent (dist_to_goal), so the metric the agent optimizes against is the same as the metric it's scored on.

Baseline benchmarks

The full benchmark slice is 57 stratified tasks — 20 easy + 20 medium + 17 hard, coast-to-coast geographic spread. The numbers below are from a 10-task validation subset (5 easy + 3 medium + 2 hard, sampled by difficulty) using v0.4 haversine scoring. Each model received the same per-turn observation channel: a 1024 × 768 image, a one-line HUD, sliding-window image history of 4. Per-task turn budget = 3 × the human's turns on that task (so an agent that needs three times as many tool calls as a human has a chance, but unbounded wandering hits a wall).

RL training

The training corpus is the full 5,958-task training split, served by the same env package. Training stack: prime-rl, single-node 8 × H100, LoRA rank 16 on the language-model blocks of Qwen3-VL-8B (vision encoder frozen — a constraint enforced by the trainer config validator), GRPO advantage with group size 8.

Key training-side decisions:

  • Sliding image-history window of 4. Each pano viewport is ~700 image tokens. With unbounded history, the prompt exceeds 32K context around turn 37. Keeping only the last 4 image observations pushes the budget past 200 turns at the cost of a small chunk of long-range visual recall.
  • Length penalty via orchestrator.length_penalty type = "turns". Replaces the hand-rolled step-efficiency term I had in the first reward draft.
  • Online difficulty filtering. Groups where every rollout got 0 reward are dropped before training. Effectively doubles compute efficiency in early training.

The headline question I want the figure to answer: does RL on the path-distance reward actually teach the model to navigate, or just teach it to game the reward (e.g. always click on the road point closest to the bottom-center pixel)? The answer hinges on how the curve looks at the medium / hard difficulty bands — easy tasks are short enough that "always click forward" is a viable degenerate policy.

Notes & choices I'd revisit

  • Real images, not synthetic. Mapillary panoramas have real-world artifacts — lens flares, motion blur, weather, signage in 50 languages — that synthetic environments smooth over. If a model can't read a STOP sign at JPEG-82 quality, that's a benchmark-worthy result.
  • Mouse-shaped action space. Asking the model to emit pixel-space click coordinates (not graph indices) forces it to actually look at the image. A model that hallucinates lat/lng is detectable.
  • Pano coverage drops abruptly at the US border. A few of the medium / hard tasks happen to clip a coastline or a county line where Mapillary coverage thins. Filtering by coverage density before sampling tasks would tighten the variance.
  • The compass currently shows true bearing. A more interesting variant would hide the compass — pure visual navigation, no metadata crutches.
  • Multi-step credit assignment is still GRPO-only. Per-turn shaping via a process reward model is the natural next experiment.
